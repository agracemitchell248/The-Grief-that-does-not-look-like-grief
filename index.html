When There's No Funeral

When someone dies, the world makes room for you. People bring food. You get time off. There are rituals... a funeral, a gathering, a card in the mail that says I see that you are hurting.

When you lose your job... or are pushed out of your career before you were ready... none of that happens.

You might get a brief email. An HR exit script. A cardboard box. Or you might get a conversation framed as "the right time," pressed on you by an organization that had already decided. And then... silence. The world keeps moving. And you're supposed to, too.

💡 Key Concept: Ambiguous Loss

Take as much time as you want to think and write

Psychologist Pauline Boss spent decades studying what she called ambiguous loss... grief for something that doesn't have a clear ending, or for a loss that the outside world doesn't fully recognize.

Job loss is a textbook example. And so is forced early retirement... perhaps even more so. When retirement is pushed on you before you were ready, the loss is real, but the cultural script works against you in a particularly cruel way: retirement is supposed to be a celebration. A reward. The finish line you've been working toward. Which means when it arrives too soon, or not on your terms, you can be left carrying grief that no one around you believes you're allowed to feel. "But you're so lucky... you can relax now." The minimization can be almost worse than the silence.

Boss found that ambiguous loss is often harder to process than clear-cut loss, precisely because it lacks the social supports and rituals that help people move through grief. You can't close a chapter that no one acknowledged was open.

"Ambiguous loss is the most devastating of losses, because it defies resolution and creates long-term confusion."

— Pauline Boss, Ambiguous Loss

Gentle reminder: If you've been wondering why you can't "just move on"... or why you're grieving something the world is calling a gift... this is part of the answer. You were never given the conditions that help people move through grief. That's not a personal failure. It's a gap in how our culture treats this kind of loss.

Who Were You There?

We've been talking about what you lost. Now I want to sit with something that often gets skipped over: who you lost.

💡 Key Concept: Identity Grief

Take as much time as you want to think and write

Your job... or your career... wasn't just a paycheck or a set of tasks. It was a context in which a version of you existed. A version who had a title, a role, a place in the room. Who knew things. Who was needed. Who belonged somewhere, every single day.

When that ended... whether through a layoff, a termination, or a retirement that came before you were ready... that version of you lost their stage. And that is a genuine grief.

For those who were pushed into early retirement, this loss can carry an additional dimension: the unfinished chapter. You still had things you wanted to do, contribute, and become. The loss isn't just of what existed... it's of what was still possible.

When It Wasn't Just a Layoff... or Just a Retirement

I want to pause here and name something that some of you are carrying that goes beyond the loss itself.

For some people, the job loss came with a specific kind of wound... not just this is over, but this was wrong. A betrayal. A broken promise. Retaliation in disguise. Being let go after years of loyalty with two weeks' notice and a generic HR email. Watching someone else take credit for your work and then watching yourself be pushed out. Being told it was "about the budget" when you knew... you knew... it was about something else entirely.

And for some people who were pushed into early retirement: the wound is that it was never actually a choice. It was framed as an opportunity, a generous offer, the right moment... but underneath it was a managed exit. The door was shown to you before you were ready to walk through it, and no one in the room acknowledged that.

If any of that is your story, what you're carrying isn't just grief. It may also be moral injury.

💡 Key Concept: Moral Injury

Take as much time as you want to think and write

Originally studied in combat veterans, moral injury describes the deep psychological wound that comes from participating in, witnessing, or being the victim of actions that violate your core moral beliefs.

In workplace and career contexts, it can look like:

  • Being laid off after being told your role was secure
  • Being managed out after raising legitimate concerns
  • Watching harmful, unethical, or discriminatory practices go unaddressed while you were penalized for objecting
  • Having your contributions erased, minimized, or taken from you
  • Being treated as disposable after years of genuine investment and sacrifice
  • Being "offered" early retirement under pressure... with the implicit message that you were no longer wanted... and having that framed as a gift
  • Being aged out of a role you were still capable of doing, with no honest acknowledgment of what was really happening

Moral injury carries a different weight than ordinary loss. It includes grief, but it also includes a rupture in the belief that the world is fair, that good work is recognized, that institutions can be trusted. That rupture takes longer to heal... and it heals differently.

Naming it doesn't mean staying in anger forever. It means acknowledging that what happened was genuinely harmful... not something you imagined, not something you need to minimize in order to "move on."

Writing Exercise: The Unsent Letter

Take as much time as you want to think and write... only go here if it feels true for you

This exercise is for people who felt a resonance with what you just read. If your loss was genuinely neutral... no betrayal, no violation underneath it... you can move on. But if something stirred, this is for you.

You're going to write a letter you will never send. It can be to a person... a manager, an executive, a board member, a colleague who stayed silent. It can be to an institution, a company, a system. It can be to whoever you believe caused this, or whoever you believe should have protected you and didn't.

A few things to consider saying: What they did. What it cost you. What you wish they understood. What you never got to say out loud.

You don't have to be fair, measured, or forgiving. This letter is for you.

  • "I need you to know what that decision actually did to me..."
  • "You called it a restructure. Here is what it really was..."
  • "You told me I was valued. I want to tell you what that word cost me when you took it back..."
  • "I built something real there. And you let me go like it didn't matter..."
  • "You framed it as an opportunity. I want to tell you what it actually felt like..."

Gentle reminder: Naming what was wrong is not the same as being trapped by it. You can hold "this was unjust" and "I am still moving forward" at the same time. Both are true. Whether this was a layoff, a termination, or a retirement you didn't fully choose... if there was a violation underneath it, that violation deserves to be named. Not buried under a job search or a retirement to-do list.

💡 When Grief Becomes Complicated

Take as much time as you want to think and write

Grief that goes on for an extended period... or grief that is significantly disrupting your daily functioning, your relationships, your physical health, or your ability to care for yourself... is sometimes called complicated grief or prolonged grief. It's not a character flaw. It doesn't mean you're broken. It means the loss hit something deep, or the conditions for processing it haven't been present.

Some signs that grief may benefit from professional support:

  • Persistent inability to function at basic daily tasks for weeks or months
  • Feeling that your future is meaningless or that you have nothing left to offer
  • Using substances to manage the pain
  • Thoughts of self-harm or not wanting to be here
  • A grief so heavy that it hasn't moved at all despite time passing
  • Social withdrawal that's becoming more and more complete

This program is not a substitute for therapy. It's a bridge... to understanding, to community, to tools. For some people, it's enough. For others, it's a starting point.

The Complexity of This Loss

Job loss and forced early retirement can carry all of this at once:

  • Ambiguous loss... a grief with no ritual, no named moment, no socially sanctioned space to mourn
  • A ritual gap... the absence of the conditions that help recognized grief actually move: acknowledgment, time, witness, ceremony
  • Identity grief... the loss not just of a job, but of a version of yourself that existed there: your title, your belonging, your purpose, your expertise
  • Moral injury... for some, a wound underneath the loss itself: a violation of what you believed was right, a betrayal that goes beyond the ending

You are not struggling because you're weak. You are struggling because you're carrying something genuinely complex... a loss that our culture under-names and under-supports, whether it came in the form of a layoff, a termination, or a retirement you didn't fully choose. You've been doing this largely without the support this kind of loss deserves.
